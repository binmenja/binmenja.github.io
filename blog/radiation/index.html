What do you mean, "Radiation"?

June 3, 2025

What I’m actually studying (and why it matters)

When people ask me what I’m researching during my Ph.D, they usually expect a simple answer. But like most scientists, my work is pretty specialized! So let me break down what “atmospheric radiation” actually means and why it’s important for understanding climate change.

Heat from Earth: The Invisible Energy We Can’t See

Think of Earth as a giant heater that’s constantly giving off energy. Just like how you feel warmth radiating from a campfire, our planet radiates energy too—but most of it is infrared radiation (heat energy that our eyes can’t see).

This invisible heat energy is crucial for Earth’s climate. Based on the typical temperatures of our planet’s surface and atmosphere, most of the thermal energy Earth gives off falls in the infrared range. This follows a fundamental physics principle called Planck’s law, which describes how hot objects emit energy:

$$B_\nu (T) = \frac{2hc^2 \nu^3}{e^{hc\nu/kT}-1} \quad (1)$$

Don’t worry about the complex math—what this equation tells us is simple: the hotter something is, the more energy it radiates. In this formula, $B_\nu$ represents how much energy is emitted at a specific wavelength, $T$ is temperature, and the other letters ($h$, $c$, $k$) are fundamental constants of physics.

But here’s the thing: Earth isn’t a perfect radiator (scientists call perfect radiators “blackbodies”). Our planet is made up of oceans, land, clouds, and various gases—each material emits energy a bit differently. So we need to account for this with a modified equation:

$$R_\nu (T) = \varepsilon_\nu \cdot B_\nu (T) \quad (2)$$

This equation includes $\varepsilon_\nu$ (called “emissivity”), which is basically a correction factor that tells us how efficiently different materials emit energy compared to a perfect radiator. Some materials are great at radiating energy, others not so much!

The Greenhouse Effect: Earth’s Natural Blanket

Now here’s where climate change comes in. Several things can change Earth’s temperature—for example, when ice melts, it exposes darker surfaces that absorb more sunlight. But the biggest driver of climate change is the greenhouse effect.

Think of greenhouse gases in our atmosphere like a blanket around Earth. These gases (like carbon dioxide and water vapor) are really good at absorbing the infrared energy that Earth tries to radiate out to space. Instead of letting this heat escape, they absorb it and re-emit it back toward Earth—essentially trapping heat in our atmosphere.

So here’s what I’m actually studying: I analyze patterns and changes in this infrared radiation to understand how our climate is changing and what’s causing these changes.

Why does this matter? Understanding these radiation patterns helps climate scientists improve their computer models, which means we can better predict future weather patterns and prepare for long-term climate changes.
