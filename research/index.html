My Academic Journey

I joined Professor Yi Huang’s research group in 2022 as a summer intern. I then stayed in the group as a Master’s student, officially starting in September 2022. After doing my coursework, I decided to fast-track to Ph.D in May 2023 as I enjoyed the environment, group, and supervisor. I also felt that a one-year research project was too short to achieve any significant research work and findings, so I decided to dedicate myself to a longer Ph.D journey.

Arctic Atmospheric Radiation

First Project: Downwelling Longwave Radiation Trends

My first project aimed at investigating the Downwelling Longwave Radiation (DLR) trends at an Arctic location based on the measurements of an Extended Atmospheric Emitted Radiance Interferometer. Essentially, this is an instrument that uses Fourier transform, the decomposition of a signal onto many harmonics, to get the radiance at different frequencies. Because this instrument has been positioned at this Arctic location for several decades now, this allows for a long-term spectrally decomposed trend analysis.

Why this matters

The Arctic is warming at about twice the global average, which is pretty significant when you think about it. By looking at these spectrally-resolved longwave radiation trends, my research helps figure out:

  • How increasing greenhouse gas concentrations are affecting downwelling radiation
  • The role of water vapor feedback in Arctic warming
  • How cloud effects are changing over time (this is where it gets really interesting!)

What we found

We constructed a long-term DLR climate record and applied a weighted linear regression trend method after classifying the spectra based on cloud conditions. We found a pan-spectral increase in DLR over the long-term period, and this trend is mainly driven by a strong enhancement of the thick/low clouds greenhouse effect.

We also found some really interesting signals, including significant changes in the far-infrared, which is particularly sensitive to the dry and cold Arctic environment.

The paper is currently under review at Journal of Geophysical Research: Atmospheres. You can find the pre-print here: Strengthening atmospheric greenhouse effect at the Arctic “North Slope of Alaska” site evidenced by long-term records of the downwelling longwave radiance spectrum.

Our results are significantly different than what my former colleague have found at a mid-latitude site: Trends in Downwelling Longwave Radiance Over the Southern Great Plains. This makes it even more interesting because it can partially explain Arctic enhanced warming.
